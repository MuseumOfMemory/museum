01 — Why this archive exists

Because without memory there is no justice.

Justice is born in memory.

It is born when someone — a mother, a brother, an entire people — refuses to forget.
When someone holds up the photo of their murdered child and says:
"This name existed. This pain is real. This crime happened."

The International Criminal Court is investigating Venezuela for crimes against humanity committed since February 12, 2014.
The United Nations documented torture, extrajudicial executions, and systematic repression.
Foro Penal recorded 17,882 arbitrary arrests.

But the names behind those numbers are at risk of being lost.
Of becoming nothing more than statistics.
Of disappearing twice — first in life, then in memory.

This memorial exists so that does not happen.

Every name recorded here is an act of justice.
Every story documented is evidence the world cannot ignore.
Every photograph preserved is a testimony that no regime can erase.

Memory is the first step toward justice.
And justice, toward freedom.

03 — Collective trauma

Because trauma needs to be seen in order to heal

There are wounds that do not bleed outward.

The families of political prisoners know what it means to fall asleep not knowing whether their loved one was tortured that night.
Former prisoners know what it means to walk out of a cell and not know how to live again.
The exiled know what it means to grieve a homeland from another continent, unable to return.
The mothers of the fallen know what it means to love — forever — someone who no longer exists.

That invisible pain is real.
That collective trauma is part of Venezuela.
And a people cannot heal what they cannot name.

This memorial is not just a historical archive.
It is a space of mourning.
It is a place where Venezuelan pain finally has a name, a face, a date.
Where families can say: "Here is our loved one. The world knows them. The world remembers them."

Because the first step to healing trauma is for someone to look you in the eyes and say:
"What was done to you was real. It matters. You are not alone."

This memorial says that.
To every victim. To every family. To every exile.
To every Venezuelan who carries the weight of what could have been.

04 — The 3 pillars of historical memory

What happened must be known

1 — Truth

A country cannot move forward without first establishing the full, documented truth of what occurred. Historical memory creates an official, permanent, irrefutable record that no regime can rewrite, deny, or erase. It names the victims, identifies the perpetrators, and establishes the timeline of violations.

"The truth is not just a right of the victims — it is an obligation of the State and a need of society."
— UN Commission on Human Rights

Without truth, crimes repeat themselves. Without a documented record, oppressors rewrite history and future generations inherit lies.

2 — Justice
"Accountability must follow"

Historical memory is the foundation of legal accountability. Every name documented, every testimony preserved, every photograph archived becomes evidence — before national courts, before the International Criminal Court, before the tribunal of history itself. Memory transforms victims into witnesses and witnesses into the driving force of justice.

"Where there is no justice, there can be no lasting peace."
— Nelson Mandela

Without memory, there is impunity. With impunity, perpetrators are emboldened to do it again. Historical memory makes impunity impossible.

3 — Non-repetition
"It must never happen again"

The deepest purpose of historical memory is not the past — it is the future. By documenting what happened, a society builds the moral and educational foundation to ensure it never happens again. It becomes a school of dignity, a warning to future generations, a compass for building free and just institutions.

"Those who cannot remember the past are condemned to repeat it."
— George Santayana

This is why the Holocaust Museum exists. This is why Mandela's Robben Island became a monument. This is why The Memory of Venezuela must exist — so that a free Venezuela is built, never again, on silence.

In one sentence:
Truth tells us what happened. Justice holds accountable those who did it. Non-repetition ensures it never happens again.

These three together are not just reasons — they are the moral obligation of every free society toward its victims.

06 — About this work

We are the only organization in the world keeping a full record of Venezuela's political prisoners, the families who wait for them, and those who have come home.

Memory of Venezuela is an independent, non-partisan archive. We do not endorse parties or candidates in any country. We document state repression as a matter of public record. Our methodology, sources, and ethics policy are published openly.
